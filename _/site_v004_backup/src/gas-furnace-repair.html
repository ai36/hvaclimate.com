FAQ and troubleshooting

Gas furnace blow cold air. What can I do?

If your gas boiler is blowing cold air, it could indicate several potential issues. Here are some steps you can take to diagnose and possibly fix the problem:

  1. Check the thermostat: Make sure the thermostat is set to heating mode and the temperature is set higher than the current room temperature.
  2. Verify programming settings: If you have a programmable thermostat, ensure it's programmed correctly for the time of day and desired temperature.
  3. Inspect air filters: Clogged filters can obstruct normal airflow, reducing heating efficiency. If filters are dirty, replace them.
  4. Check vents: Ensure all vents and registers are open and not blocked by furniture or drapes.
  5. Look for airflow blockages: Check external air ducts for any obstructions or clogs that could hinder air circulation.
  6. Restart the system: Sometimes, resetting the boiler can fix a temporary issue. Turn off the boiler's power for a few minutes, then turn it back on.
  7. Inspect the burner flame: If you have access and knowledge, check to see if the boiler's burner flame is lit. A non-lit flame could indicate a gas supply problem or a burner malfunction.
  8. Check the heating system for leaks: Leaks in the heating system can lead to heat loss and insufficient heating.

If the problem persists after these steps or you're unsure about performing them, it's strongly recommended to contact a professional heating technician for service and repair. A technician can accurately diagnose the issue and suggest the appropriate repairs. Attempting to intervene without proper knowledge and skills can be dangerous.

Gas furnace leaking water. What should you do first?

If your gas furnace is leaking water, it's important to address the issue promptly to prevent further damage to your furnace and your home. Here's what you should do first:

  1. Turn Off the Power to the Furnace: Safety first. Before inspecting or touching your furnace, ensure that the power is turned off. This can usually be done at the furnace switch or the main electrical panel.
  2. Identify the Source of the Leak: Try to locate where the water is coming from. Common sources of leaks in a gas furnace include the condensation line, the humidifier (if your furnace is equipped with one), or the heat exchanger.
    • Condensation Leak: High-efficiency gas furnaces produce condensation which is drained away from the unit. A clogged condensation drain or a break in the line can cause leaks.
    • Humidifier Leak: If your furnace has an integrated humidifier, it might be leaking water due to a clog or malfunction.
    • Heat Exchanger Leak: This is more serious, as it could indicate a crack in the heat exchanger, which can lead to a carbon monoxide leak.
  3. Contain the Leak: If possible, try to contain the leak by placing a bucket or towels to catch the water. This can help minimize water damage to your home while you work on a more permanent solution.
  4. Inspect and Clear the Condensate Line: If the leak is coming from the condensate line, check for any clogs or blockages. You can try to clear the line yourself using a wet/dry vacuum on the line's exterior end.
  5. Check the Furnace Filter: A dirty furnace filter can restrict airflow, causing the coil to freeze and then leak water when it melts. If the filter is dirty, replace it.
  6. Call a Professional: Unless the solution is simple and safe, like changing a dirty filter or unclogging a condensation line, it's best to call a professional HVAC technician. This is especially true if you suspect the leak is coming from the heat exchanger or if you can't identify the source of the leak.

Remember, regular maintenance of your furnace can help prevent issues like water leaks. Make sure to have your furnace inspected and serviced by a professional annually.

Gas furnace making noise

If your gas furnace is making noise, it could indicate several issues. Here are some common causes of noise and how you can address them:

  1. Dirty Filter: A clogged filter can restrict airflow, causing excessive pressure and noise in the system. Check and replace the filter if it's dirty.
  2. Fan Issues: Worn fan bearings or debris in the fan can cause noise. Inspect the fan for damage and consult a specialist for replacement or repair if necessary.
  3. Heat Exchanger Problems: Cracks or holes in the heat exchanger can not only cause noise but also pose a serious danger due to carbon monoxide leaks. Immediately contact a qualified technician to check and replace the heat exchanger if needed.
  4. Clogged Condensate Lines: The condensate from high-efficiency gas furnaces must be removed through special lines. Clogging of these lines can cause noise. Check and clean the condensate lines.
  5. Improper Installation: If the furnace is improperly installed or if there are issues with the ductwork, it can cause noise during operation. Contact a specialist to check and adjust the installation.
  6. Wear and Tear: Over time, some worn-out furnace parts may begin to make noise. Regular maintenance will help identify and replace worn parts before they cause problems.

General Recommendations:

  • Regular Maintenance: Regular checks and maintenance can prevent many issues, including noise.
  • Professional Diagnosis: If you're unsure of the cause of the noise or how to fix it, it's best to seek help from a professional HVAC technician.

Note that ignoring the noise can lead to further damage to the equipment and even pose a safety hazard. Therefore, it's important not to delay addressing the issue.

Gas furnace not working. What to check before calling a specialist?

Before calling a professional for a non-working gas furnace, here are a few things you can check:

  1. Thermostat Settings: Ensure the thermostat is set to "heat" and the temperature setting is higher than the current room temperature.
  2. Power Supply: Check if the furnace switch is turned on. Also, verify that the circuit breaker hasn’t tripped or a fuse hasn’t blown.
  3. Furnace Filter: A dirty filter can restrict airflow and cause the furnace to not operate properly. Check and replace the filter if necessary.
  4. Pilot Light (for older furnaces): If your furnace has a pilot light, make sure it’s lit. If it’s out, follow the manufacturer's instructions to relight it.
  5. Gas Supply: Verify that the gas valve to the furnace is fully open. Also, check if other gas appliances in the house are working to rule out a gas supply issue.
  6. Air Vents and Returns: Make sure all air vents and returns are open and not obstructed to ensure proper airflow.
  7. Furnace Door: Ensure the furnace door is properly closed. Some furnaces have a safety switch that prevents operation if the door is open.

If you have checked these items and the furnace still does not work, it may be time to call a professional. Regular maintenance can also prevent many issues from occurring.

Gas furnace smells gas — urgent action

If you smell gas coming from your gas furnace, it's a serious safety hazard that requires immediate attention. Here’s what you need to do without delay:

  1. Do not attempt to turn on or off any electrical switches, appliances, or lights. This could trigger an explosion.
  2. Evacuate everyone from the building immediately. Do not try to locate the source of the gas leak inside.
  3. As you leave, open doors and windows to ventilate the area if it is safe to do so without delaying your exit.
  4. Once you are in a safe location, call your gas company's emergency number or the fire department to report the gas leak. Use a phone outside the house to avoid igniting the gas.
  5. Do not re-enter the building until it has been inspected and declared safe by professionals.
  6. If you know how to do it safely, turn off the gas supply valve to your home before leaving. This valve is typically located near the gas meter.

Remember, a gas leak is a serious risk to your safety and health, potentially leading to fires, explosions, or carbon monoxide poisoning. Always take immediate action and let professionals handle the situation.
